The Great Charter

What Is Magna Carta?

A charter of liberties agreed to by King John of England at Runnymede on June 15, 1215. It was drafted by rebellious barons to limit royal power and protect their rights. Written in Latin on parchment, sealed with the royal seal.

It Failed — Then Succeeded

The original Magna Carta was annulled by the Pope after just ten weeks. But it was reissued, revised, and confirmed dozens of times over the following centuries. Its ideas proved impossible to kill.

Revolutionary Ideas

For the first time in English history, a king was forced to accept that nobody — not even the monarch — is above the law. The charter established principles of due process, trial by jury, and limits on taxation.

Global Impact

Magna Carta influenced the U.S. Constitution, the Bill of Rights, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, and legal systems worldwide. It is one of the most important legal documents in human history.

"No free man shall be seized or imprisoned... except by the lawful judgment of his equals or by the law of the land." — Magna Carta, Clause 39

King John — The Worst King in English History?

John Lackland (r. 1199–1216)

John was the youngest son of Henry II — a man described as cruel, treacherous, and incompetent. He lost Normandy to the French king, was excommunicated by the Pope, and taxed his barons mercilessly to fund failed wars. By 1215, the barons had had enough.

Crushing Taxation

John imposed enormous taxes (scutage) to pay for his disastrous military campaigns in France. He also seized baronial lands, sold heiresses in marriage, and demanded huge ransoms. The barons were being bled dry.

Conflict with the Church

John quarreled with Pope Innocent III over the appointment of the Archbishop of Canterbury. The Pope placed England under interdict — no sacraments, no marriages, no burials in consecrated ground. John was excommunicated, then cynically surrendered England to the Pope and received it back as a papal fief.

Rebellion

In 1215, the barons rose in revolt. They captured London — and the city's support was decisive. John, isolated and outmaneuvered, was forced to negotiate. The result was Magna Carta, sealed at Runnymede meadow near Windsor.

Key Clauses That Changed the World

39

Due Process of Law

"No free man shall be seized or imprisoned... except by the lawful judgment of his equals or by the law of the land." The foundation of habeas corpus and the right to a fair trial — still cited in courts today.

40

Justice for All

"To no one will we sell, to no one deny or delay right or justice." A promise that justice would not be for sale — a radical idea in a world where the rich could buy favorable judgments.

12

No Taxation Without Consent

Taxes could not be imposed without the "general consent of the realm" — a council of barons and bishops. This is the distant ancestor of parliamentary control over taxation.

14

Council of the Realm

Required the king to summon a council of barons and church leaders to consent to taxation. This council evolved into Parliament — one of the world's oldest representative assemblies.

61

The Security Clause

A council of 25 barons was empowered to enforce the charter — by seizing the king's castles and lands if necessary. This was revolutionary: a mechanism to hold the king accountable. It was also the clause that got the charter annulled.

Annulment, Death & Resurrection

Immediate Failure

John had no intention of honoring Magna Carta. He appealed to Pope Innocent III, who declared the charter "null and void of all validity forever" in August 1215 — just ten weeks after it was sealed. Civil war resumed.

John's Death (1216)

John died of dysentery in October 1216 — likely from overeating peaches and drinking too much cider. His nine-year-old son, Henry III, became king. The regents reissued a revised Magna Carta to rally support against the French invaders.

Reissues & Confirmations

Magna Carta was reissued in 1216, 1217, and definitively in 1225. It was confirmed dozens of times by English monarchs over the following centuries. By the 14th century, it had become a symbol of the law's supremacy over the king.

Parliamentary Development

The council of the realm evolved into the English Parliament. The principle that the king needed consent to tax became the foundation of parliamentary power — and, eventually, constitutional monarchy.

Global Legacy

United States

The Founding Fathers cited Magna Carta extensively. The Fifth Amendment ("due process of law") and the Constitution's limits on executive power directly echo the charter.

Rule of Law

Magna Carta established the principle that everyone — including the ruler — is subject to the law. This is the bedrock of modern constitutional government.

Human Rights

The Universal Declaration of Human Rights (1948) echoes Magna Carta's language on fair trial and justice. The charter is recognized as a foundational human rights document.

Surviving Copies

Four original 1215 copies survive — two in the British Library, one at Lincoln Cathedral, one at Salisbury Cathedral. They are among the most treasured documents in the world.

"Magna Carta is the foundation of the freedom of the individual against the arbitrary authority of the despot." — Lord Denning, celebrated British judge
