The Rise of the Knight

Origins

Knights emerged in the 8th–10th centuries as heavily armored mounted warriors. The stirrup (introduced from Asia) made fighting from horseback effective. Frankish knights under Charles Martel proved decisive at the Battle of Tours (732).

Expensive Profession

Being a knight required enormous wealth: a warhorse cost as much as a small farm, armor was custom-made, and training took years. Only nobles could afford the full equipment — creating a warrior aristocracy.

Becoming a Knight

Training began at age 7 as a page (learning manners and basic skills), then as a squire (serving a knight, learning combat) from about 14. The accolade — dubbing ceremony — usually occurred around age 21.

Tournaments

Mock battles where knights could practice, gain fame, and win prizes — including ransoms from captured opponents. The joust (one-on-one mounted combat with lances) became the most prestigious event. Tournaments were both sport and deadly serious training.

The Code of Chivalry

Christian Warrior Ethic

Chivalry blended martial prowess with Christian virtues. Knights swore to defend the Church, protect the weak, and fight for justice. The ideal knight was pious, loyal, courageous, and merciful.

Courtly Love

A literary and social ideal: the knight devoted himself to a noble lady (often married and unattainable), performing heroic deeds in her honor. Troubadours sang of this idealized love, which shaped Western ideas of romance.

Reality vs. Ideal

The chivalric code was often honored in the breach. Knights could be brutal: slaughtering peasants, breaking oaths, and fighting for plunder. The ideal of chivalry was as much about controlling knights as inspiring them.

Arthurian Legend

King Arthur and the Knights of the Round Table became the ultimate expression of chivalry. Stories of Lancelot, Gawain, Galahad, and the quest for the Holy Grail spread across Europe and still shape our image of knighthood.

"A knight should be brave and loyal, courteous and generous, a defender of the Church and protector of the weak." — The idealized code of chivalry

Armor & Weapons

Chainmail (Hauberk)

Thousands of interlocking iron rings. Flexible, strong against slashing attacks, but heavy (up to 15 kg). Worn over padded gambeson.

Plate Armor

Evolved in the 14th–15th centuries. Full suits weighed 20–25 kg — but distributed weight allowed surprising mobility. A knight in plate could mount his horse and run.

Great Helm

Early enclosed helmet with narrow eye slits. Later replaced by visored helmets like the bascinet and sallet for better visibility and breathing.

Longsword

Double-edged blade, 90–110 cm long. Used for cutting and thrusting. The sword was also a symbol of knighthood — often blessed at the altar before dubbing.

Lance

The primary weapon of the mounted knight. A long wooden shaft (3–4 m) with a metal tip, couched under the arm for maximum impact in the charge.

Poleaxe & Mace

Blunt-force weapons designed to crush plate armor. The mace and war hammer could dent helmets and break bones even through steel plates.

The Age of Castles

Purpose

Castles were fortified residences — home to the lord and his family, administrative center of the fief, and military stronghold. They projected power over the surrounding countryside and served as refuges during war.

Motte-and-Bailey

The earliest castle type (10th–11th centuries): a wooden tower on an artificial mound (motte) with an enclosed courtyard (bailey) below. Quick to build and effective, these spread across Norman-conquered England.

Stone Keeps

Massive stone towers — the White Tower of London, Rochester Castle, Château de Coucy. Walls up to 7 meters thick. The keep was the last line of defense and the lord's residence.

Concentric Castles

The pinnacle of castle design (late 13th century): multiple rings of walls, round towers (no blind spots), and elaborate gatehouses. Krak des Chevaliers and Caerphilly Castle are prime examples. They were extraordinarily difficult to capture.

Tower of London
England, built 1078 by William the Conqueror

The most famous castle in England — royal palace, prison, treasury, and symbol of Norman power.

Krak des Chevaliers
Syria, built 12th century by Crusaders

Perhaps the finest medieval castle ever built. Its concentric defenses were considered impregnable.

Château Gaillard
France, built 1196 by Richard the Lionheart

Richard's "saucy castle" — an advanced design on a commanding cliff overlooking the Seine.

Neuschwanstein
Germany, built 19th century

Not medieval but the ultimate romantic vision of a knight's castle — built by "Mad" King Ludwig II of Bavaria.

The End of the Knight & Castle

Gunpowder

Cannons could breach stone walls. The age of castles ended. Fortifications evolved into low, thick-walled star forts designed to absorb cannon fire — no longer homes but purely military structures.

Infantry Revolution

Well-trained infantry with pikes and longbows (like Swiss pikemen and English archers) could stop cavalry charges. Knights were no longer invincible on the battlefield.

Romantic Revival

In the 19th century, knights and castles were romanticized. Medieval tales were retold, mock castles were built, and the ideal of chivalry was revived — influencing everything from Boy Scouts to modern fantasy literature.

Enduring Symbols

The knight remains a powerful symbol of courage, honor, and service. The castle endures as an icon of mystery, romance, and a lost world. From Disney to Game of Thrones, they still capture our imagination.

"A knight in shining armor is a man who has never had his metal truly tested." — Modern proverb reflecting both the ideal and reality
