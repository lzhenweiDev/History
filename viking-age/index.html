The Northmen Cometh

The Viking Age Begins

Traditionally dated from the raid on Lindisfarne monastery in 793 AD — "Never before has such terror appeared in Britain," wrote the monk Alcuin. The age ended with the Norman Conquest of England in 1066.

Who Were the Vikings?

Scandinavians from modern Norway, Sweden, and Denmark. They weren't a unified people but shared language (Old Norse), religion (Norse gods), and culture. "Viking" was an activity — to go raiding — not an ethnicity.

The Longship

The key to Viking success. Sleek, fast, and shallow-drafted, longships could cross oceans and sail up rivers. They could land on beaches without ports — striking anywhere, anytime, with terrifying speed.

Why Did They Raid?

Multiple factors: overpopulation in Scandinavia, political centralization pushing out rivals, the lure of undefended monasteries full of treasure, and improved ship technology. Climate warming may have boosted population and ambition.

"From the fury of the Northmen, O Lord, deliver us." — Traditional prayer, though likely apocryphal

Raids & Conquests

793 Lindisfarne

Vikings sacked the monastery of Lindisfarne off the coast of Northumbria. Monks were killed or enslaved. The attack sent shockwaves through Christian Europe — "the heathens have poured out the blood of saints."

845 Siege of Paris

Ragnar Lothbrok (legendary Viking hero) led 120 ships up the Seine and besieged Paris. The French king paid 7,000 pounds of silver to make them leave — the first major Danegeld (protection money).

865 The Great Heathen Army

A massive Viking army invaded England, led by the sons of Ragnar Lothbrok: Ivar the Boneless, Halfdan, and Ubba. They conquered Northumbria, East Anglia, and much of Mercia within years.

911 Normandy Founded

The French king granted land to the Viking leader Rollo — creating the Duchy of Normandy. Rollo's descendants, the Normans, would conquer England (1066) and southern Italy. "Norman" means "Northman."

Exploration & Discovery

Iceland

Settled by Norse around 870 AD. Became a thriving republic with the Althing — the world's oldest surviving parliament.

Greenland

Erik the Red founded colonies around 985 AD. The Norse lived there for over 400 years before mysteriously vanishing.

Vinland (America)

Leif Erikson reached North America around 1000 AD — 500 years before Columbus. L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland confirms the Norse presence.

The Volga Route

Swedish Vikings (Varangians) sailed down Russian rivers to the Black and Caspian Seas, trading with Byzantium and the Islamic Caliphate.

Kievan Rus

Vikings founded the first Russian state. The name "Russia" likely derives from "Rus" — the name given to these Scandinavian settlers.

Varangian Guard

Viking warriors served as the elite bodyguard of the Byzantine Emperor in Constantinople. They were renowned for their loyalty and ferocity.

Viking Society & Culture

Norse Religion

Vikings worshipped a pantheon of gods: Odin (wisdom, war), Thor (thunder, protection), Freyja (love, fertility), and Loki (trickster). Valhalla was the hall of slain warriors. By the 11th century, most had converted to Christianity.

Runes & Sagas

Vikings used the runic alphabet (Futhark) for inscriptions, memorials, and magic. The Norse sagas — written in Iceland in the 13th century — preserve their myths, history, and legends.

Women's Rights

Viking women had more rights than most medieval European women: they could own property, inherit, divorce their husbands, and manage farms. The shield-maiden legend — while debated — reflects a culture where women could be formidable.

The Thing

Viking assemblies where free men gathered to settle disputes, make laws, and judge crimes. The Icelandic Althing (founded 930 AD) is the world's oldest continuous parliament. Democracy has deep roots in the North.

"Cattle die, kinsmen die, the self must also die; but glory never dies, for the one who is able to achieve it." — Hávamál, the Sayings of the High One (Odin)

Legacy of the Vikings

Genetic Heritage

Viking DNA is found across Britain, Ireland, Iceland, Normandy, and Russia. Millions of people — especially in the British Isles and Scandinavia — carry Norse ancestry.

Language

English words like "sky," "window," "knife," "husband," "egg," "berserk," and days of the week (Thursday = Thor's day) come from Old Norse. Place names ending in -by, -thorpe, and -toft mark Viking settlements.

Legal & Political

The jury system, the Althing, and the idea of law as something made by the people (not kings) owe much to Norse traditions. The Danelaw in England shaped English common law.

Modern Fascination

From Wagner's operas to Marvel's Thor, from the TV series Vikings to video games — the Viking image of the fierce, free warrior continues to captivate the modern imagination.
