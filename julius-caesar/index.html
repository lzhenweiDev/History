Julius Caesar

Conqueror, Dictator, Legend · 100–44 BC

The man who ended the Roman Republic and paved the way for the Empire — one of history's most brilliant and controversial figures

The Man Who Would Be King

Noble Birth

Born in 100 BC to the patrician gens Julia, which claimed descent from the goddess Venus and the Trojan hero Aeneas. Despite his noble lineage, his family was not particularly wealthy or powerful.

Genius & Ambition

A brilliant military commander, charismatic politician, skilled orator, and accomplished writer. Caesar combined talents rarely found in one person — and ambition that knew no bounds.

Dictator Perpetuo

After winning the civil war, Caesar was appointed dictator for life in 44 BC. This unprecedented concentration of power terrified the Roman elite — and sealed his fate.

Calendar Reformer

Caesar reformed the Roman calendar in 46 BC, creating the Julian calendar with 365 days and a leap year every four years. It remained in use for over 1,600 years — and is the basis of our modern calendar.

"Veni, vidi, vici." — "I came, I saw, I conquered." — Julius Caesar, after his lightning victory at Zela, 47 BC

Rise to Power

69 BC Early Career

Caesar climbed the cursus honorum — the ladder of Roman political offices. He served as quaestor in Spain, aedile in Rome (throwing spectacular games that won popular favor), and pontifex maximus (chief priest).

60 BC The First Triumvirate

Caesar formed a secret alliance with Pompey the Great (Rome's greatest general) and Crassus (Rome's richest man). Together they dominated Roman politics, sidelining the Senate.

59 BC Consul of Rome

As consul, Caesar pushed through legislation benefiting his allies and himself — often ignoring senatorial opposition and constitutional norms. He secured the governorship of Gaul for an unprecedented term.

The First Triumvirate

Pompey provided military prestige, Crassus provided money, and Caesar provided political cunning. This unofficial alliance dominated Rome for seven years — until Crassus died in Parthia and Pompey turned against Caesar.

The Gallic Wars (58–50 BC)

Conquest of Gaul

Caesar conquered all of Gaul (modern France, Belgium, and parts of Switzerland) in a series of brilliant campaigns. He invaded Britain twice and bridged the Rhine to strike into Germania.

The Cost

Ancient sources claim over one million Gauls were killed and another million enslaved. The conquest was brutal — but it made Caesar enormously wealthy and gave him a battle-hardened army loyal to him personally.

Commentarii

Caesar wrote his own account: Commentarii de Bello Gallico (Commentaries on the Gallic War). Written in elegant, clear Latin, it served as both history and propaganda — still used to teach Latin today.

Siege of Alesia (52 BC)

Caesar's masterpiece. He besieged the Gallic leader Vercingetorix while simultaneously building fortifications to hold off a relief army. The double siege succeeded — and Gaul was permanently subdued.

"Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres." — "All Gaul is divided into three parts." — Opening of Caesar's Commentaries

Civil War & Dictatorship

49 BC Crossing the Rubicon

The Senate, led by Pompey, ordered Caesar to disband his army. Instead, Caesar crossed the Rubicon River into Italy with his legion — an act of treason. "Alea iacta est" — "The die is cast." Civil war began.

48 BC Battle of Pharsalus

Caesar decisively defeated Pompey's larger army in Greece. Pompey fled to Egypt, where he was murdered on the orders of the young pharaoh Ptolemy XIII. Caesar wept when presented with Pompey's head.

48–47 BC Cleopatra & Egypt

Caesar became entangled in Egyptian politics, siding with Cleopatra VII against her brother. Their alliance — and love affair — produced a son, Caesarion. Caesar placed Cleopatra on the throne.

46 BC Master of Rome

After mopping up remaining opposition in Africa and Spain, Caesar celebrated four magnificent triumphs. He was now the undisputed ruler of the Roman world.

The Ides of March

King in All But Name

Caesar accumulated unprecedented powers: dictator for life, consul, tribune. He placed his image on coins, wore the purple robe of kings, and accepted divine honors. Many feared he would crown himself king.

The Conspirators

Over 60 senators joined the plot, led by Brutus (whom Caesar had pardoned and favored) and Cassius. They called themselves "The Liberators" and claimed to be restoring the Republic.

March 15, 44 BC

Caesar was stabbed 23 times in the Theatre of Pompey during a Senate meeting. According to legend, his last words to Brutus were "Kai su, teknon?" — "You too, my child?" He died at the foot of Pompey's statue.

Aftermath

The assassins expected to be hailed as heroes. Instead, they were forced to flee. Caesar's adopted heir Octavian (later Augustus) and Mark Antony pursued them — and the Republic descended into another civil war that ended with the Empire's creation.
