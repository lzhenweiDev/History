The Black Death

The Great Pestilence · 1347–1351

The deadliest pandemic in human history — a catastrophe that killed up to half of Europe's population and changed the world forever

The Great Mortality

Unimaginable Scale

Between 1347 and 1351, the Black Death killed an estimated 75–200 million people across Eurasia and North Africa. Europe lost 30–60% of its total population. No event before or since has killed such a proportion of humanity.

What Was It?

The disease was bubonic plague, caused by the bacterium Yersinia pestis. It spread primarily through fleas living on black rats, but also through airborne transmission (pneumonic plague) — the most deadly form.

Not the First — Not the Last

The Black Death was the second great plague pandemic. The first was the Plague of Justinian (541–542 AD). A third pandemic began in China in the 1850s. The bacterium still exists today — but antibiotics can now treat it.

Why "Black"?

The name "Black Death" came later — 14th-century people called it "The Great Mortality" or "The Pestilence." "Black" may refer to the dark bruising and tissue death (gangrene) that appeared on victims' bodies.

The March of Death

1330s Origins in Asia

The plague likely originated in the steppes of Central Asia or in China. It spread along the Silk Road trade routes, carried by fleas on rats in merchant caravans. Mongol armies may have helped spread it.

1346 Siege of Caffa

The Mongols besieged the Genoese trading port of Caffa in Crimea. When plague broke out in their ranks, they catapulted infected corpses over the walls — one of history's earliest recorded acts of biological warfare.

1347 Arrival in Europe

Genoese ships fleeing Caffa brought the plague to Messina, Sicily, in October 1347. From there, it spread like wildfire: to Marseille, Genoa, Venice, and throughout the Mediterranean.

1348 Peak Devastation

The plague swept through France, Spain, and England. Paris lost half its population. In Florence, Boccaccio wrote: "How many valiant men... how many fair ladies... breakfasted with their kinfolk and that same night supped with their ancestors."

1349–1351 Northern Europe & Beyond

The plague reached Germany, Scandinavia, and Eastern Europe. It also devastated the Middle East and North Africa. By 1351, the first wave subsided — but outbreaks recurred for centuries.

The Disease

High Fever

Sudden onset of extreme fever, chills, and weakness. Victims were often bedridden within hours of first symptoms.

Buboes

Painful, swollen lymph nodes (buboes) in the groin, armpit, or neck — sometimes growing to the size of an apple. The word "bubonic" comes from these.

Pneumonic Form

When the infection reached the lungs, it became airborne — spread by coughing. This form was 100% fatal without modern treatment and killed within 1–2 days.

Tissue Death

Fingers, toes, and nose tips turned black from gangrene (tissue death). Blood vessels burst, causing dark bruising under the skin — the "black" in Black Death.

"The symptoms were not such as they are in the East, where a flow of blood from the nose was a manifest sign of inevitable death; but both men and women... were afflicted with swellings in the groin or armpits." — Giovanni Boccaccio, The Decameron

How People Responded

Religious Explanations

Many believed the plague was God's punishment for sin. The Flagellants roamed Europe, publicly whipping themselves to atone. Some turned to piety; others to hedonism — "eat, drink, and be merry, for tomorrow we die."

Scapegoating: Jews

Jews were falsely accused of poisoning wells to cause the plague. Across Europe, Jewish communities were massacred. In Strasbourg (1349), 2,000 Jews were burned alive. Pope Clement VI condemned the violence — but few listened.

Plague Doctors

The iconic beak-like mask worn by plague doctors was filled with herbs and spices to "purify" the air — a primitive theory of contagion. The wooden cane was used to examine patients without touching them.

Quarantine

Venice established the first quarantine system. Ships arriving from plague-affected ports were required to wait 40 days (Italian: quaranta — giving us the word "quarantine") before docking. It actually helped slow the spread.

A Transformed World

Labor Shortage

With so many dead, labor became scarce and valuable. Peasants could demand higher wages and better conditions. Serfdom declined sharply in Western Europe. The power balance between lords and workers shifted permanently.

Crisis of Faith

The Church's inability to stop the plague — and the death of many priests who stayed to minister to the sick — weakened its authority. This contributed to the conditions that sparked the Reformation a century later.

Art & Literature

The plague left a deep mark on culture. The Danse Macabre (Dance of Death) motif — showing Death leading all people, rich and poor alike — became widespread. Boccaccio's Decameron was set during the plague.

Genetic Legacy

Recent research suggests the Black Death may have shaped human genetics. Survivors passed on immune system genes that may have helped their descendants resist the plague — but may also increase susceptibility to autoimmune diseases today.

Birth of Public Health

The need to understand and control the plague led to early forms of public health: quarantine, sanitation improvements, and the beginnings of epidemiology. These responses laid groundwork for modern disease control.

Never Really Gone

The plague bacterium still exists in rodent populations worldwide. Isolated cases still occur — including in the United States. But today, antibiotics make it treatable. The Black Death taught us lessons we still use in fighting pandemics.

"In the year of our Lord 1348, there occurred in Florence the most deadly pestilence... It was spread by contact with the sick, for it would pass from one person to another." — Giovanni Boccaccio, The Decameron
