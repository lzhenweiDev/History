Alexander the Great

Conqueror of the Known World · 356–323 BC

The young Macedonian king who forged one of history's largest empires in just 13 years — and changed the world forever

The Legend

Empire Size

From Greece to India, Alexander's empire stretched over 5 million square kilometers — one of the largest empires in ancient history, spanning three continents.

Short Reign

Alexander ruled for only 13 years (336–323 BC), yet in that time he conquered the mighty Persian Empire, reached the Indus River, and never lost a single battle.

Bucephalus

Alexander's beloved horse, which he tamed as a boy when no one else could. The horse carried him through countless battles. When Bucephalus died, Alexander named a city after him.

Aristotle's Pupil

Tutored by the great philosopher Aristotle from age 13 to 16. Alexander carried a copy of Homer's Iliad on his campaigns — he saw himself as a new Achilles.

Early Life & Rise to Power

Birth & Omens

Born in 356 BC in Pella, Macedonia. Legends say the Temple of Artemis burned down on his birth night — because the goddess was attending his birth.

Philip II

His father transformed Macedonia from a backwater into Greece's dominant power. Philip's military reforms — the sarissa pike and the Macedonian phalanx — gave Alexander his unstoppable army.

Assassination

In 336 BC, Philip II was assassinated at his daughter's wedding. Alexander, aged 20, immediately seized the throne — and ruthlessly eliminated all rivals.

Thebes Destroyed

When Thebes rebelled, Alexander razed the city to the ground, sparing only the temples and the house of the poet Pindar. Greece never challenged him again.

"My father will have nothing left to conquer!" — Young Alexander, reportedly complaining about Philip's military successes

334 BC Crossing into Asia

Alexander crossed the Hellespont with 37,000 men. He visited Troy, honoring Achilles, before defeating a Persian army at the Granicus River — his first major victory.

333 BC Battle of Issus

Alexander defeated Darius III in person, forcing the Persian king to flee and leave behind his family, whom Alexander treated with respect. The famous "Alexander Mosaic" depicts this battle.

332 BC Siege of Tyre

An island fortress that had never been taken. Alexander built a causeway to reach it — a feat of engineering still visible today. After seven months, Tyre fell.

331 BC Battle of Gaugamela

The decisive battle. Darius III fielded an army perhaps five times larger, with scythed chariots and war elephants. Alexander's tactical genius shattered the Persian center — Darius fled, and the Persian Empire collapsed.

330 BC Burning of Persepolis

The ceremonial capital of Persia was burned — whether deliberately or in a drunken revel is debated. It symbolized the end of Achaemenid power and Greek vengeance for the Persian burning of Athens.

326 BC Into India

Alexander crossed the Indus River and defeated King Porus at the Hydaspes. Impressed by Porus' bravery, Alexander made him an ally. But his exhausted army refused to go further — and Alexander finally turned back.

Key Battles & Tactics

The Hammer & Anvil

Alexander's signature tactic: the phalanx (anvil) pinned the enemy, while the Companion Cavalry (hammer) struck the decisive blow at a weak point — usually led by Alexander himself.

Sarissa Phalanx

The Macedonian infantry wielded sarissas — pikes up to 6 meters long. This wall of spear points was nearly impenetrable from the front, holding enemies in place for the cavalry charge.

Companion Cavalry

Elite heavy cavalry, Alexander's personal shock troops. They charged in wedge formation, targeting gaps and the enemy commander — Alexander always led from the front.

Adaptability

Alexander never used the same tactic twice. Against Scythian horse archers, mountain fortresses, war elephants, and scythed chariots — he always found a way to win.

"I am not afraid of an army of lions led by a sheep; I am afraid of an army of sheep led by a lion." — Attributed to Alexander the Great

Death & Enduring Legacy

Mysterious Death

Alexander died in Babylon in 323 BC, aged 32. The cause remains debated: poison, malaria, typhoid, or complications from old wounds. His body was preserved in honey.

Fragmented Empire

Without a clear heir, his generals (the Diadochi) fought for decades, splitting the empire into Hellenistic kingdoms: Ptolemaic Egypt, Seleucid Asia, and Antigonid Macedonia.

Hellenistic World

Greek language, culture, art, and philosophy spread across the known world. Koine Greek became the common language — the language of the New Testament and early Christianity.

Alexandria

The greatest of Alexander's 70+ cities. Alexandria in Egypt housed the Great Library and the Pharos Lighthouse — becoming the intellectual capital of the ancient world for centuries.

A Bridge Between Worlds

Alexander's conquests connected Europe, Asia, and Africa in unprecedented ways. Trade routes opened, ideas mixed, and the fusion of Greek and Eastern cultures — Hellenism — laid foundations for the Roman Empire and Western civilization itself.

"There is nothing impossible to him who will try." — Alexander the Great
