The Hundred Years' War

England vs. France · 1337–1453

The longest conflict in European history — a dynastic struggle that transformed two nations and ended the age of chivalry

116 Years of War

Not Quite 100 Years

Despite its name, the war lasted 116 years (1337–1453) — but it was not continuous. It was a series of campaigns, truces, and renewed hostilities across five generations.

Dynastic Conflict

At its core was the question: who had the right to the French throne? English kings claimed descent from French royalty. When the Capetian dynasty died out in 1328, Edward III of England pressed his claim.

Territorial Disputes

English kings still held large territories in France — the Duchy of Aquitaine (Guyenne). French kings sought to absorb these lands into the royal domain. Conflict was inevitable.

Birth of Nations

The war transformed England and France from feudal collections of territories into nation-states with distinct identities, languages, and patriotic feeling. "England for the English" was born.

Roots of Conflict

The Succession Crisis (1328)

Charles IV of France died without a male heir. Edward III of England was his nephew through his mother Isabella. French nobles rejected Edward's claim, choosing Philip of Valois instead — citing Salic Law forbidding female succession.

Aquitaine & Gascony

English kings were also vassals of the French crown for their lands in southwestern France. This awkward feudal relationship — a king bowing to another king — created endless friction.

Economic Rivalry

England's wool trade with Flanders was vital. When France threatened Flanders, England intervened. Control of the Channel and access to continental markets were at stake.

French Raids (1337)

Philip VI confiscated Aquitaine and launched raids on the English coast. Edward III responded by formally claiming the French throne — and war began.

Joan of Arc — The Maid of Orléans

The Peasant Girl Who Saved France

A 17-year-old illiterate peasant from Domrémy who claimed divine voices told her to drive the English from France. She convinced the Dauphin (the future Charles VII) to give her command. Her arrival at Orléans in 1429 broke the English siege and turned the tide of the war.

1429 Victory at Orléans

Joan arrived with supplies and inspired the French soldiers. Within nine days, the English siege was broken. It was the first major French victory in a generation.

1429 Coronation of Charles VII

Joan escorted the Dauphin through enemy territory to Reims, where he was crowned king in the traditional cathedral — a tremendous symbolic victory that legitimized his claim.

1430 Capture & Trial

Joan was captured by Burgundians (allied with England) and sold to the English. She was put on trial for heresy and witchcraft — a political trial dressed in religious robes.

1431 Execution & Sainthood

Burned at the stake in Rouen on May 30, aged 19. Her ashes were thrown into the Seine. She was canonized as a saint in 1920 — the patron saint of France.

Consequences & Legacy

End of Chivalry

The longbow and gunpowder made armored knights obsolete. Crécy, Poitiers, and Agincourt proved that common archers could defeat noble cavalry — democratizing warfare.

Rise of Nationalism

England and France emerged as unified nations with distinct identities. English replaced French as the language of the English court. Patriotism was born.

Constitutional Development

Edward III needed Parliament to fund the war. In exchange, Parliament gained power over taxation — a crucial step toward English constitutional monarchy.

France Transformed

France emerged stronger: royal authority was consolidated, the nobility weakened, and a standing army created. The foundations of absolute monarchy were laid — culminating in Louis XIV.

Wars of the Roses

Defeat in France destabilized England. Returning soldiers and nobles turned on each other, sparking the Wars of the Roses (1455–1487) — a bloody civil war between Lancaster and York.

Cultural Memory

Shakespeare's history plays, countless novels, and films keep the war alive in imagination. Joan of Arc remains one of history's most compelling figures — saint, warrior, martyr.

"The Hundred Years' War was the crucible in which the English and French nations were forged." — Modern historical consensus
