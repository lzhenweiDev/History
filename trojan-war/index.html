The Legendary War

Troy

A wealthy, fortified city located in what is now northwestern Turkey, near the Dardanelles strait. Troy controlled vital trade routes between the Aegean and the Black Sea.

Homer's Epics

The war is primarily known through Homer's Iliad and Odyssey, composed around the 8th century BC. The Iliad covers only a few weeks in the tenth year of the war.

The Greek Alliance

Mycenae, Sparta, Athens, Ithaca and other Greek city-states united under Agamemnon's leadership to sail a thousand ships against Troy.

Ten Years of War

According to legend, the siege lasted ten long years, ending only through the famous stratagem of the Trojan Horse.

"Sing, O Muse, of the wrath of Achilles..." — Opening line of Homer's Iliad

What Sparked the War?

The Golden Apple

Eris, goddess of discord, threw a golden apple inscribed "To the Fairest" among goddesses Hera, Athena, and Aphrodite. Paris of Troy was chosen to judge.

The Judgment of Paris

Aphrodite promised Paris the most beautiful woman in the world — Helen of Sparta — in exchange for being chosen as the fairest goddess.

Helen's Abduction

Paris traveled to Sparta, where King Menelaus hosted him. Paris either seduced or abducted Helen and fled back to Troy, violating sacred guest-friendship.

Oath of Tyndareus

All of Helen's former suitors had sworn an oath to defend her marriage. When Menelaus called upon them, the Greek kings were bound to join the expedition against Troy.

Heroes & Warriors

Achilles

The greatest Greek warrior. His mother Thetis dipped him in the River Styx, making him nearly invincible — except for his heel. His rage and withdrawal form the core of the Iliad.

Agamemnon

King of Mycenae and commander-in-chief of the Greek forces. His conflict with Achilles over the captive Briseis nearly cost the Greeks the war.

Hector

Prince of Troy and its greatest defender. A noble warrior and loving family man, he was ultimately slain by Achilles in single combat outside the walls of Troy.

Odysseus

King of Ithaca, renowned for his cunning. He devised the Trojan Horse strategy. His ten-year journey home is the subject of Homer's Odyssey.

Key Events of the War

The Gathering Greek Fleet Assembled at Aulis

Over 1,000 ships gathered. Agamemnon was forced to sacrifice his daughter Iphigenia to appease Artemis for favorable winds.

Year 1–9 The Long Siege

Greeks besieged Troy. Battles raged on the plains outside the walls. The Trojans, aided by allies, successfully defended their city for years.

The Wrath Achilles Withdraws

Agamemnon seized Briseis from Achilles. Enraged, Achilles refused to fight. Without him, the Greeks suffered devastating losses.

The Duel Death of Hector

After Hector killed his beloved friend Patroclus, Achilles returned to battle with fury. He slew Hector and dragged his body behind his chariot.

The Fall The Trojan Horse

Odysseus devised a giant wooden horse filled with Greek warriors. The Trojans brought it inside their walls. At night, the Greeks emerged and destroyed Troy.

Historical Truth & Legacy

Schliemann's Discovery

In the 1870s, German archaeologist Heinrich Schliemann excavated the site of Hisarlik in Turkey, believing it to be Homer's Troy. Multiple layers of settlement were found.

Troy VIIa

Archaeologists identified Troy VIIa (c. 1260–1180 BC) as the most likely candidate for Homeric Troy, showing evidence of destruction by warfare.

Enduring Stories

The Trojan War has inspired countless works: from ancient Greek tragedies to Shakespeare's Troilus and Cressida, to modern films like Troy (2004).

Language & Phrases

"Achilles' heel" (fatal weakness), "Trojan Horse" (deceptive trap), and "the face that launched a thousand ships" all derive from this legend.

"There is no greater sorrow than to recall a happy time in misery." — Dante, reflecting on Trojan War tales
