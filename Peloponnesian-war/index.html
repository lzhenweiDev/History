Greece's Great Civil War

Athens & The Delian League

An empire of over 150 city-states paying tribute to Athens. The world's first democracy commanded the strongest navy in Greece, with wealth from silver mines and imperial tribute.

Sparta & The Peloponnesian League

A militaristic society where every male citizen was a professional soldier. Sparta led a coalition of land powers, feared for their unbeatable hoplite phalanx.

Timeline

431–404 BC — Twenty-seven years of intermittent warfare across Greece, Sicily, and the Aegean. The war ended with Athens' surrender and the fall of its empire.

Thucydides' Account

The Athenian general Thucydides wrote the definitive history of the war, pioneering objective historical analysis. His work remains essential reading in military academies and political science.

Origins of the Conflict

Athenian Imperialism

After the Persian Wars, Athens transformed the Delian League from a defensive alliance into an empire. Allies who tried to leave were brutally punished.

Spartan Fear

Thucydides wrote: "What made war inevitable was the growth of Athenian power and the fear which this caused in Sparta." This "Thucydides Trap" still describes great power rivalry today.

Corinthian Rivalry

Corinth, Sparta's powerful ally, felt threatened by Athenian expansion into its sphere of influence. Conflicts over Corcyra and Potidaea pulled both great powers toward war.

The Megarian Decree

Athens imposed economic sanctions on Megara, a Spartan ally, excluding them from all Athenian-controlled ports and markets. Sparta demanded its repeal — Athens refused.

"It was the rise of Athens and the fear that this instilled in Sparta that made war inevitable." — Thucydides, History of the Peloponnesian War

The Three Phases of War

431–421 BC The Archidamian War

Sparta invaded Attica annually, burning crops while Athenians retreated behind their Long Walls. Pericles' strategy relied on naval raids. The plague of Athens (430 BC) killed Pericles and a third of the population. Ended with the fragile Peace of Nicias.

415–413 BC The Sicilian Expedition

Athens' disastrous attempt to conquer Syracuse. Alcibiades, the expedition's architect, defected to Sparta after being recalled for scandal. The entire Athenian fleet and army were destroyed — the turning point of the war.

413–404 BC The Decelean War (Ionian War)

Sparta occupied Decelea in Attica permanently, cutting off Athens' silver mines. With Persian gold funding a Spartan fleet under Lysander, Athens was finally starved into surrender after the Battle of Aegospotami (405 BC).

Key Figures

Pericles (Athens)

The architect of Athenian greatness. His strategy of naval dominance and defensive walls might have succeeded — but the plague took him in 429 BC. His Funeral Oration defined Athenian ideals.

Alcibiades (Athens / Sparta / Persia)

The most controversial figure of the war: brilliant, charming, and utterly untrustworthy. He switched sides three times — from Athens to Sparta to Persia and back — profoundly shaping the war's course.

Lysander (Sparta)

Spartan admiral who built a navy with Persian funding and decisively defeated Athens at Aegospotami. He blockaded the Piraeus and forced Athens' final surrender in 404 BC.

Cleon (Athens)

A populist demagogue who rose after Pericles' death. He advocated brutal policies — including the proposed massacre of all Mytilenean men. Killed in battle at Amphipolis in 422 BC.

Brasidas (Sparta)

Sparta's most dynamic general. He marched north and successfully detached several Athenian allies. Killed alongside Cleon at Amphipolis, his death removed a major obstacle to peace.

Consequences & Legacy

End of Athens' Golden Age

The Long Walls were torn down to the music of flutes. The Athenian Empire dissolved. Though Athens recovered some independence, it never regained its former power and prosperity.

Democracy Overthrown

Sparta installed the "Thirty Tyrants," an oligarchic regime that executed 5% of Athenian citizens. Though democracy was restored within a year, the trauma was profound.

The Thucydides Trap

Modern political scientists use the term to describe how a rising power (like China today) and an established power (like the USA) can slide into war through fear and miscalculation.

Weakened Greece

The war exhausted all Greek city-states, leaving them vulnerable. Within decades, Philip II of Macedon would conquer the weakened poleis, paving the way for Alexander the Great.

"The strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must." — Thucydides, the Melian Dialogue
