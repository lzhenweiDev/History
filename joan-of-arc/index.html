Joan of Arc

The Maid of Orléans · 1412–1431

The peasant girl who heard voices, led armies, saved a kingdom — and became a saint burned at the stake at nineteen

The Maid from Domrémy

Humble Beginnings

Born around 1412 in Domrémy, a small village in northeastern France. Her parents were peasants. She could not read or write. She spun wool, tended animals, and was known as a pious, hardworking girl — utterly ordinary, until she was not.

France in Crisis

When Joan was born, France was losing the Hundred Years' War. The English and their Burgundian allies controlled much of the country. The Dauphin (crown prince) Charles had not been crowned — his legitimacy was in doubt.

A Brief, Brilliant Career

Joan's public life lasted barely two years — from her first audience with the Dauphin in 1429 to her death in 1431. In that time, she transformed a hopeless war and became a legend.

The Mystery

How did an illiterate teenage peasant girl convince a desperate prince to give her command of an army? How did she win battles experienced commanders had lost? These questions have fascinated historians for centuries.

"I am not afraid. I was born to do this." — Joan of Arc

The Voices & Visions

Saint Michael

The archangel appeared first, telling 13-year-old Joan to be pious and that saints would guide her. He was the protector of France — a powerful symbol.

Saint Catherine

Patron saint of young girls and virgin martyrs. She and Margaret were the primary voices guiding Joan's mission.

Saint Margaret

Patron saint of childbirth and protector against demons. Together with Catherine, she gave Joan detailed instructions.

What Did They Say?

The voices told Joan she had a divine mission: to drive the English from France, to lift the siege of Orléans, and to escort the Dauphin to Reims for his coronation. She was to be God's instrument.

Explanations

Were Joan's voices divine revelation? Schizophrenia? Epilepsy? Religious ecstasy? Modern scholars have proposed various explanations — but none fully explain her military success and political acumen. The mystery endures.

The Campaign

February 1429 Meeting the Dauphin

Joan traveled through enemy territory to the Dauphin's court at Chinon. She identified Charles — reportedly disguised among courtiers — and told him: "I am sent by God to help you and your kingdom." After theological examination, she was given armor, a banner, and troops.

April–May 1429 Relief of Orléans

The siege had lasted seven months. Joan arrived with supplies and inspiration. Within nine days, the English were driven back. Joan — wounded by an arrow — returned to the fight. The victory was seen as miraculous.

June 1429 The Loire Campaign

Joan led French forces to a series of stunning victories: Jargeau, Meung-sur-Loire, Beaugency. At the Battle of Patay, the English army was destroyed — French knights slaughtered English longbowmen before they could set up positions.

July 1429 Coronation at Reims

Joan escorted the Dauphin through enemy territory to Reims. Charles VII was crowned in the traditional cathedral, with Joan standing beside him holding her banner. "Gentle king," she wept, "now is fulfilled the pleasure of God."

Capture, Trial & Martyrdom

Capture (May 1430)

Defending Compiègne, Joan was pulled from her horse by Burgundian soldiers. She was sold to the English for 10,000 livres — a king's ransom. The English needed to discredit her: if she was sent by God, their claims to France were illegitimate.

The Trial (1431)

Joan was tried by an ecclesiastical court at Rouen — controlled by the English. The charges: heresy and witchcraft. The real crime: wearing men's clothing and claiming direct communication with God without the Church's mediation. The trial was politically motivated and procedurally rigged.

Testimony

Joan's trial transcripts survive — and they are extraordinary. An illiterate teenager faced the greatest legal minds of her time and answered with wit, courage, and clarity. When asked if she was in God's grace, she replied: "If I am not, may God put me there; and if I am, may God so keep me."

Execution (May 30, 1431)

Condemned as a relapsed heretic for resuming male clothing, Joan was burned at the stake in the marketplace of Rouen. She was 19 years old. She asked for a cross to be held before her eyes as the flames rose. Witnesses said she called "Jesus" as she died. Her ashes were scattered in the Seine.

"I hold everything from God. I have done nothing but by the will of God." — Joan of Arc at her trial

Vindication & Sainthood

1456 The Nullification Trial

After the war ended, Pope Callixtus III authorized a retrial. Witnesses were called, evidence reviewed. Joan was declared innocent. The original trial was condemned as "corruption, deceit, slander, and fraud."

1909 Beatification

Joan was beatified — the first step toward sainthood. France had long venerated her as a national heroine; now the Church formally recognized her holiness.

1920 Canonization

Pope Benedict XV canonized Joan as a saint. The peasant girl who was burned as a heretic became Saint Joan of Arc — patron saint of France, soldiers, and prisoners.

In Culture

Joan has inspired countless works: plays by George Bernard Shaw and Jean Anouilh, films by Dreyer and Besson, operas, novels, and paintings. She is one of the most depicted figures in Western art.

Feminist Icon

Joan defied every expectation of her gender in the 15th century: she led armies, advised kings, and spoke with authority. She is both a saint and a symbol of female empowerment.

National Symbol

Joan is the national heroine of France. Her image — in armor, holding her banner — appears on statues, medals, and monuments across the country. She represents French resilience and faith.

Enduring Inspiration

Joan's story transcends nationality and religion: a young person of humble origin who changed the world through courage and conviction. Her words still resonate: "I am not afraid. I was born to do this."

"One life is all we have and we live it as we believe in living it. But to sacrifice what you are and to live without belief, that is a fate more terrible than dying." — Joan of Arc (as portrayed in literature)
